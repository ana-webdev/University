The mask question

About six in ten people who are back in offices say that they have been asked to wear masks, the Moneypenny survey reports. About a third of the respondents said they had no problem with this, but another third said that they would not want to spend the entire working day wearing one. Do masks work? Dr Jenna Macciochi, an immunologist at the University of Sussex, says: “In light of what we know now I think they are a sensible addition to other measures. A systematic review of masks in the community — albeit not homemade ones — shows they slightly reduced the chance of contracting Covid when both infected and uninfected people wear them.”

Air transmission

The biggest question about office-air safety is whether Covid-19 can spread only through large droplets, such as spittle and sneezes, or waft invisibly over distances greater than a metre through aerosols suspended in the air. Until recently, the World Health Organisation (WHO) decreed aerosol transmission impossible. Now, after months of expert pressure, including a letter signed by 239 scientists, the WHO has reversed its position, acknowledging that it may happen. Paul Lynch, an airflow-modelling specialist at the London-based engineering consultancy Arup, says that aerosol transmission would explain incidents such as a coronavirus outbreak that struck 94 workers on the 11th floor of a South Korean skyscraper in March.
